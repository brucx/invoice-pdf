What to put on a freelance invoice

  • A real invoice number. Use a running sequence like INV-0007 — clients' accounting departments need it, and it makes your bookkeeping (and tax season) far easier.
  • Hourly work as quantity × rate. Put hours in the quantity column and your rate as the unit price: "UX design — 12 × $85". The template multiplies it out so the math is never questioned.
  • Fixed-price items as quantity 1. "Logo package — 1 × $1,200" sits happily next to hourly lines on the same invoice.
  • A due date and payment terms. "Due within 14 days" in the notes, plus your bank, PayPal, or Wise details, removes every excuse for a late payment.
  • Tax, if you charge it. Set your VAT/GST/sales-tax percentage once and it's applied to the subtotal automatically.

Example freelance invoice line items

A typical freelance web-project invoice built with this template might look like: "Discovery & wireframes — 8 × $95", "Visual design — 24 × $95", "Development — 1 × $3,600", followed by a 10% repeat-client discount and local tax. Enter those five numbers and the finished PDF shows a subtotal, the discount, tax, and a bold total due — no spreadsheet needed.

Tips for getting paid faster

Send the invoice the same day you deliver the work, while it's top of mind. Keep payment terms short — 14 days beats 30 unless the client's process demands otherwise. Put the exact payment method in the notes so there's zero friction. And keep your numbering consistent: a professional-looking, correctly-totaled PDF invoice signals that you track your receivables and will follow up.
